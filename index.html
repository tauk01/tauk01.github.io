Introduction

We have been using Spotify the past year to listen to our favorite music and explore new artists who I'd never come across if it wasn't for their recommendation system. Spotify is one of the top music streaming apps of our generation with a library of millions of songs and playlists. Recently, we found out that the app classifies all its tracks according to a system of audio features, and that it stores them in a database easily accessible through its API. So we though, if we could use this data to get a better understanding of what makes a song popular and make predictions of what the next biggest hit requires in order to have a chance at standing at the top. For our tutorial, we will be exploring the relationship between popular music and features that Spotify's API has in order to discover any trends based on subgroups of music divided on things like popularity or year it has been released.

Let's take a look at the most popular songs from 2010 to 2019 and see if we can find any trends that can help us make accurate predictions about the future of music.

According to the Spotify website, all of their songs are given a score in each of the features and characteristics of a song like their:

  • Loudness
  • Speechiness
  • Danceability
  • Valence
  • Energy
  • Tempo
  • Instrumentalness
  • Liveness
  • Acousticness

All of these features will be described in depth later in this process.

The data that we are using comes from this Kaggle dataset: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/leonardopena/top-spotify-songs-from-20102019-by-year

Goal: Predict what's popular music in the next decade based on top music from 2010-2019

We are now looking at these features in the dataset

  • Beats per minute (bpm): This describes the estimated tempo of the track in bpm. Tempo is described the speed or pace of a given piece and derives directly from the average beat duration.
  • Energy (nrgy): Energy is a measure from 0 to 100 and represents an intuitive measure of the intensity and activity of a track. Typically, energetic tracks feel fast, loud, and noisy like death metal would be classified high energy, while a Bach prelude would have a lower energy rating.
  • Danceability (dnce): Danceability describes how suitable a track is for dancing based on a combination of musical elements including tempo, rhythm stability, and beat strength. A value closer to 0 means the track is harder to dance to and a value closer to 100 means the track is easy to dance to.
  • Loudness (dB): The overall loudness of a track is measured in decibels (dB) and the values are averaged across the full track which is useful for comparing relative loudness between tracks. Loudness is the feature of a sound that correlates to the physical strength or amplitude. Values typical range between -60 and 0 db
  • Liveness (live): Detects the presence of an audience in the recording. Higher liveness values represent an increased probability that the track was performed live. A value above 0.8 provides strong likelihood that the track is live.
  • Valence (val): A measure from 0 to 100 describing the musical positiveness conveyed by a track. Tracks with high valence sound more positive (e.g. happy, cheerful), while tracks with low valence sound more negative (e.g. sad, depressed, angry).
  • Acousticness (acous): A track is consiedered acoustic when it solely or primarily uses instruments that produce sound through acoustic means, as opposed to electronic means of amplification.

Using a least squares regression, we want to check if we would reject the null hypothesis of no relationship between popularity and other features that we are focusing on. By using an alpha value of 0.1 and comparing that to the p-values of all the factors to see which ones have a statistically signifigant relationship with popularity. We can see that danceability, loudness, and liveness show a statistically signifigant with popularity which makes sense since these factors are common in a majority of popular songs. For all the other features, we would fail to reject the null hypothesis which means that there doesn't appear to be a statistically signifigant relationship between popularity and the other independent variables since they have a higher p-value than the alpha value that we're using.

Given that loudness had the lowest p-value, so we hypothesize it to have the highest effect on popularity in the upcoming decade.
